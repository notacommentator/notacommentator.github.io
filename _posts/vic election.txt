The Worst Election Ever (or, the Uninspiring, the Ungovernable and the Unbearable)

It is difficult to think of an election in Australian history like this, where every key party is an uninspiring or unpleasant choice.

A Bit of History

The ALP came to power in 2014 after one term in opposition, defeating the  Baillieu-Napthine government in a fairly narrow victory. The Coalition had won the previous election with the narrowest of majorities and were later reduced to a minority government, so the swing was not huge. It's also worth noting Tony Abbott was Prime Minister which was a drag on the Coalition.

In the 2018 election, Premier Daniel Andrews increased his lead substantially and Labor generally maintained strong approval ratings throughout his term (excluding perhaps the brief period of the 'African gangs' scare campaign). The following election was of course the first post-COVID election: 2022. This period marked a significant shift in the perception of Andrews, where he became viewed as a controlling figure, both by the public and within his own party. He reportedly had a very top-down approach to governing: his ministers were seen as almost bureaucrats who carried our his will rather than active participants in decision-making. Regardless, he was up against a very disorganised and disunited opposition headed by an unlikeable leader. Not to mention that, where it counted, Labor had shown themselves to be capable and hard-working. Sure, they were probably excessively strict with lockdowns, but that showed decisive action in light of the Morrison government's abrogation of responsibility. Andrews had also achieved a lot of infrastructure investment which is always popular.

Consequently, Labor won with an increased majority despite suffering a swing against them. It seemed that Andrews was untouchable. Which made it all the more surprising that, after promising to carry out his term to completion, he stepped down as leader in 2023. The real reason for this was speculated to be so that he could put his preferred successor in place (not helping assuage the 'dictator Dan' accusations). This was Jacinta Allan, Minister for Transport and Infrastructure and Deputy Leader of the party. It is very important to remind readers that the ALP is governed by factions: to prevent discord between the left and right factions, leadership positions and ministries are carefully handed out. Allan was a member of Andrew's faction, the left, and so in order to placate the right, who had been waiting their turn to lead, the position of Deputy Leader was given to Ben Carroll.

The Allan Government

When Allan began her premiership, there was a narrative that she was planning to bring about a new way of governing: being more consultative with her ministers and relinquishing some of the centralisation of power that had occurred under Andrews. While this may have been the case, the government's actual plans and direction remained largely the same. There was still a large focus on infrastructure and there was still no significant attempts to cut spending to help service the state's debt. Often it has felt like this is a government making decisions based on what would endear voters to them rather than based on what is best for the state in the long-term. This has shown in a few different ways, from little things like free public transport for under-18s and seniors (before that got extended to everyone for a while) to the decision to push for work-from-home legislation which was quite explicitly made to be used as a wedge issue at the election.

Allan, it should be noted, represents a seat that is becoming quite marginal and which will definitely be a battleground over the campaign between Labor, the Nationals and One Nation (and likely independents). The seat is Bendigo East, a relatively rural seat, and its demographics have been viewed as influencing Allan's actions, such as her opposition to gun reform and her refusal to ban duck shooting, both of which have drawn the ire of more progressive voters.

The state's infrastructure agenda, the Big Build, was the Andrews government's signature policy and it's come under increased scrutiny as time has gone on. Some of the projects, particularly the Suburban Rail Loop (SRL), have dubious business cases or are viewed as having been prioritised ahead of more important projects because of their electoral benefit (i.e. that it's just pork barreling). This has given the impression of a profligate government, spending billions of money on things that are 'nice to have' instead of more pressing, less flashy areas (e.g. repairing roads). Now, the state debt is less of an issue than some people would have you believe. After all, government debt and household debt are two very different things. If the money is spent effectively on projects that end up improving the productivity of the state, or if the projects provide their own revenue, then the debt can be quite easily serviced over the long term provided the economy grows. That is a bit of a big if. Regardless, good infrastructure is more important than low debt, in my view, and I don't particularly hold any grudges over the Big Build agenda.

The bigger issue, however is the corruption in the building industry and the unions. Like I said, debt is fine if the money is spent _effectively_
